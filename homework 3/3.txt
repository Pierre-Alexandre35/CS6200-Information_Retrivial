This is a list of costliest Atlantic hurricanes, broadly capturing the severity of the damage each system has caused. The costliest tropical cyclone on record in the Atlantic is held jointly by hurricanes Katrina and Harvey, both of which resulted in approximately $125 billion in property damage during the year they occurred.
A majority of the costliest Atlantic hurricanes in recorded history have peaked as major hurricanes. However, weaker tropical cyclones can still cause widespread damage. Tropical storms Alberto in 1994, Allison in 2001, Lee in 2011 and Imelda in 2019 caused over a billion dollars in damage. Flooding typically accounts for about 60% of all of a storm's damages,[citation needed] and this is reflected in the list with Allison, Harvey, and Florence and most recently Imelda which produced catastrophic rainfall; and with Katrina, Ike, and Sandy which produced devastating storm surges. Wind damage encompasses a large portion of storm damage as well, evidenced by Andrew, Irma, and Michael. Due to their excessive damage, the names of tropical cyclones accruing at least $1 billion in damage are usually retired by the World Meteorological Organization, but this is not always the case. Hurricane Juan in 1985 was the first hurricane to cause at least a billion in damage and not be retired; its name was retired on a later usage in 2003 that did not cause over a billion in damage. Since Juan, eight tropical cyclones that caused at least a billion in damage were not retired, the most notable of which Hurricane Karl which incurred $3.9 billion in damage, and the most recent of which being Hurricane Isaac in 2012.
The first hurricane to cause at least $1 billion in damage was Hurricane Betsy in 1965, which caused much of its damage in southeastern Louisiana. Four years later, Hurricane Camille caused over $1 billion in damage as it ravaged Louisiana and Mississippi at landfall, and Virginia after moving inland. After the 1960s, each decade saw an increase in tropical cyclones causing at least a billion in damage over the last, due to increasing urban development and population. In the 1970s, four hurricanes caused at least a billion in damage; the costliest of which was Hurricane Agnes, which caused $2.1 billion in damage. The following decade featured seven hurricanes causing at least a billion in damage. In the 1990s, twelve tropical cyclones accrued at least a billion in damage, including Hurricane Andrew in 1992. The system greatly exceeded the damage figure of any preceding tropical cyclone, causing $27.3 billion in damage, mostly in South Florida. Nineteen tropical cyclones in the 2000s caused at least $1 billion in damage. The 2005 season had six billion-dollar hurricanes, the most of any season on record. Hurricanes Ivan in 2004 and Irma in 2017 caused at least $1 billion in damage in four separate countries. Thus far in the 2010s, twelve storms have caused at least $1 billion in damage, the most recent of which is Tropical Storm Imelda, which accrued at least $5 billion in damage[1].
This list ranks tropical cyclones within the Atlantic that have accrued at least US$1 billion in damage, based on their nominal USD damage totals. Because these numbers have not been adjusted for inflation, nor adjusted for changes in population and wealth in coastal counties, the costliness of recent hurricanes is artificially inflated compared to hurricanes of the past. This makes it hard to accurately compare the damage inflicted by hurricanes over time.

id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>\n\t\t<div id="contentSub"></div>\n\t\t\n\t\t\n\t\t<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>\n\t\t<a class="mw-jump-link" class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Wikimedia list article</div>\n<p class="mw-empty-elt">\n</p>\n<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hurricane_katrina_damage_gulfport_mississippi.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" in <a href="/wiki/Long_Beach,_Mississippi" title="Long Beach, Mississippi">Long Beach, Mississippi</a> following <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">Hurricane Katrina</a>.</div></div></div>\n<p>This is a <b>list of costliest Atlantic hurricanes</b>, broadly capturing the severity of the damage each system has caused. The costliest tropical cyclone on record in the Atlantic is held jointly by hurricanes <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">Katrina</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Harvey" title="Hurricane Harvey">Harvey</a>, both of which resulted in approximately $125 billion in property damage during the year they occurred.\n</p><p>A majority of the costliest Atlantic hurricanes in recorded history have peaked as major hurricanes. However, weaker tropical cyclones can still cause widespread damage. Tropical storms <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Alberto_(1994)" title="Tropical Storm Alberto (1994)">Alberto</a> in <a href="/wiki/1994_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1994 Atlantic hurricane season">1994</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Allison" title="Tropical Storm Allison">Allison</a> in <a href="/wiki/2001_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2001 Atlantic hurricane season">2001</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Lee_(2011)" title="Tropical Storm Lee (2011)">Lee</a> in <a href="/wiki/2011_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2011 Atlantic hurricane season">2011</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Imelda" title="Tropical Storm Imelda">Imelda</a> in <a href="/wiki/2019_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2019 Atlantic hurricane season">2019</a> caused over a billion dollars in damage. Flooding typically accounts for about 60% of all of a storm\'s damages,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> and this is reflected in the list with <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Allison" title="Tropical Storm Allison">Allison</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Harvey_(2017)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hurricane Harvey (2017)">Harvey</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Florence" title="Hurricane Florence">Florence</a> and most recently <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Imelda" title="Tropical Storm Imelda">Imelda</a> which produced catastrophic rainfall; and with Katrina, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Ike" title="Hurricane Ike">Ike</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Sandy" title="Hurricane Sandy">Sandy</a> which produced devastating storm surges. Wind damage encompasses a large portion of storm damage as well, evidenced by <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Andrew" title="Hurricane Andrew">Andrew</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Irma" title="Hurricane Irma">Irma</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Michael" title="Hurricane Michael">Michael</a>. Due to their excessive damage, the <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_naming" title="Tropical cyclone naming">names</a> of tropical cyclones accruing at least $1 billion in damage are usually <a href="/wiki/List_of_retired_Atlantic_hurricane_names" title="List of retired Atlantic hurricane names">retired</a> by the <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">World Meteorological Organization</a>, but this is not always the case. <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Juan_(1985)" title="Hurricane Juan (1985)">Hurricane Juan</a> in <a href="/wiki/1985_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1985 Atlantic hurricane season">1985</a> was the first hurricane to cause at least a billion in damage and not be retired; its name was retired on a <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Juan" title="Hurricane Juan">later usage</a> in <a href="/wiki/2003_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2003 Atlantic hurricane season">2003</a> that did not cause over a billion in damage. Since Juan, eight tropical cyclones that caused at least a billion in damage were not retired, the most notable of which <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Karl" title="Hurricane Karl">Hurricane Karl</a> which incurred $3.9 billion in damage, and the most recent of which being <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Isaac_(2012)" title="Hurricane Isaac (2012)">Hurricane Isaac</a> in <a href="/wiki/2012_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2012 Atlantic hurricane season">2012</a>.\n</p><p>The first hurricane to cause at least $1 billion in damage was <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Betsy" title="Hurricane Betsy">Hurricane Betsy</a> in <a href="/wiki/1965_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1965 Atlantic hurricane season">1965</a>, which caused much of its damage in southeastern <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a>. Four years later, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Camille" title="Hurricane Camille">Hurricane Camille</a> caused over $1 billion in damage as it ravaged Louisiana and Mississippi at landfall, and Virginia after moving inland. After the 1960s, each decade saw an increase in tropical cyclones causing at least a billion in damage over the last, due to increasing urban development and population. In the 1970s, four hurricanes caused at least a billion in damage; the costliest of which was <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Agnes" title="Hurricane Agnes">Hurricane Agnes</a>, which caused $2.1 billion in damage. The following decade featured seven hurricanes causing at least a billion in damage. In the 1990s, twelve tropical cyclones accrued at least a billion in damage, including <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Andrew" title="Hurricane Andrew">Hurricane Andrew</a> in <a href="/wiki/1992_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1992 Atlantic hurricane season">1992</a>. The system greatly exceeded the damage figure of any preceding tropical cyclone, causing $27.3 billion in damage, mostly in <a href="/wiki/South_Florida" title="South Florida">South Florida</a>. Nineteen tropical cyclones in the 2000s caused at least $1 billion in damage. The <a href="/wiki/2005_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2005 Atlantic hurricane season">2005</a> season had six billion-dollar hurricanes, the most of any season on record. Hurricanes <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Ivan" title="Hurricane Ivan">Ivan</a> in 2004 and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Irma" title="Hurricane Irma">Irma</a> in 2017 caused at least $1 billion in damage in four separate countries. Thus far in the 2010s, twelve storms have caused at least $1 billion in damage, the most recent of which is <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Imelda" title="Tropical Storm Imelda">Tropical Storm Imelda</a>, which accrued at least $5 billion in damage<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>.\n</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overall_costliest">Overall costliest</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_costliest_Atlantic_hurricanes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overall costliest">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>This list ranks tropical cyclones within the Atlantic that have accrued at least <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">US</a>$1 billion in damage, based on their <a href="/wiki/Real_versus_nominal_value_(economics)" title="Real versus nominal value (economics)">nominal</a> USD damage totals. Because these numbers have not been adjusted for inflation, nor adjusted for changes in population and wealth in coastal counties, the costliness of recent hurricanes is artificially inflated compared to hurricanes of the past. This makes it hard to accurately compare the damage inflicted by hurricanes over time.\n</p><p>In contrast, the normalized damage reported in the adjacent column gives an estimate of the direct economic losses from each hurricane if that same event was to occur under contemporary societal conditions, based on a method by <a href="/wiki/Roger_A._Pielke_Jr." title="Roger A.
